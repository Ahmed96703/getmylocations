About GPS coordinate formats

Every point on Earth's surface can be expressed as a pair of angles measured from the equator (latitude) and from the prime meridian in Greenwich (longitude). The pair is the same physical location no matter which format you write it in — the formats simply differ in how the fractional part of each angle is broken up.

Decimal Degrees (DD) is the modern default. It writes the angle as a single signed number with a fractional part: 48.858420. Most navigation apps, GPS chips, mapping APIs, and Google Maps URLs use this format. It's the easiest to copy, paste, and store in a spreadsheet.

Degrees, Minutes, Seconds (DMS) splits each degree into 60 minutes and each minute into 60 seconds: 48° 51' 30.3" N. Hemisphere letters (N / S / E / W) replace the negative sign. DMS still appears on older nautical charts, aviation publications, and most land-survey documents.

Degrees and Decimal Minutes (DDM) is the format favored by marine GPS units and by amateur radio operators. It keeps the degree as an integer and writes the minute as a decimal: 48° 51.5052' N. It's a natural intermediate between DD and DMS.

Six decimal places of latitude or longitude in DD give about one meter of precision — already finer than what most consumer GPS chips can deliver under normal sky conditions. Three decimals are enough for a city block, four for a building, five for a parking space. Beyond six, the extra digits are noise.

Two common pitfalls when converting by hand: the order matters (latitude always comes first in human-readable output, though GeoJSON and some libraries flip it), and the hemisphere letters and the negative sign are alternatives, not stacked. 48° 51' N and +48° 51' mean the same thing; writing -48° 51' S is double-negation and almost always a typo.

Frequently asked questions

How accurate are the coordinates my browser reports?

On a phone outdoors with a clear sky, modern GPS / GNSS chips deliver roughly 3–5 meters of accuracy. Indoors, on a desktop, or behind tall buildings, the operating system falls back to Wi-Fi positioning (10–25 m) or cell-tower / IP triangulation (kilometers). Each browser reading includes an accuracy value in meters — treat it as the radius of a circle the device believes it's inside.

Why does the converter show different signs and hemisphere letters?

Decimal Degrees uses signs (positive for North and East, negative for South and West). DMS and DDM replace the sign with a hemisphere letter. The converter handles both conventions for you — if you enter a negative DD, the DMS hemisphere flips automatically; if you change the hemisphere selector, the DD field becomes negative.

Can I use this offline?

The conversion math runs entirely in your browser, so once the page is loaded the converter itself works without an internet connection. The interactive map underneath needs to load tile images from OpenStreetMap, so it requires a connection to display correctly.

Does GetMyLocations store the coordinates I enter?

No. All conversions and the optional current-location lookup happen in your browser. We do not transmit your coordinates to a server we operate. Map tiles are fetched from OpenStreetMap, which has its own privacy practices. See our Privacy Policy for the full breakdown.
