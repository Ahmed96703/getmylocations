How to use this tool

  1. Enter origin. Type any address, landmark, business name, or paste a coordinate pair like 31.5497, 74.3436. Click Use my location to start from where you are.
  2. Enter destination. Same — any address or coordinate.
  3. Pick travel mode. Driving is the default. Walking, bicycling, and public transit are also available.
  4. Click Get directions. The route appears on a map with estimated time and distance. Turn-by-turn steps are visible in the embedded view; click Open in Google Maps for the full interactive route.

About route planning

Modern routing engines combine three sources of data: a road graph (which streets connect to which intersections, in which direction, with what speed limits), historical traffic data (typical speed at this time of day on this segment), and real-time traffic feeds (where vehicles are reported slow right now). The engine searches for the path with the lowest predicted travel time, not the shortest distance.

This is why two routes between the same two points can differ — one shorter in kilometers, the other faster in minutes. It is also why directions sometimes send you on a longer-looking detour: the algorithm knows a closer route is currently jammed.

Public-transit routing is much harder than driving. The engine needs schedules, transfers, walking-segment timing, and live disruption data — and the result has to update as soon as a bus is late or a train is canceled. Most cities outside major capitals only have basic transit support; small towns may have none.

For walking and biking, the engine uses pedestrian-safe paths where available — sidewalks, pedestrian crossings, bike lanes. Walking directions sometimes route through parks or staircases that aren't obvious from a driving map.

Frequently asked questions

Can I add waypoints (multi-stop routes)?

This embed supports a single origin and destination. For multi-stop routes, click Open in Google Maps and add waypoints there.

Does it show live traffic?

The embed shows the same traffic-adjusted ETA Google Maps shows. Color overlays vary by region.

Why is my walking ETA so different from Google Maps?

Walking ETAs assume an average pace of about 5 km/h on flat terrain. Hills, stairs, traffic lights, and accessibility constraints can make real walks substantially slower.

Does this work offline?

No. The embed loads tiles and route data from Google's servers on demand. Use the official Google Maps app for offline directions.
