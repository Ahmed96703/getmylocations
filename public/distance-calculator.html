How to use this calculator

  1. Get Point A coordinates. Either click Use my location to fill it from your device, or paste a latitude / longitude pair (decimal degrees) you already have.
  2. Enter Point B coordinates. Type any second pair — a destination, a landmark, the address of a vacation rental.

  4. Try other pairs. Edit either set of coordinates and recalculate — the map redraws each time.

What is "great-circle distance"?

The great-circle distance is the shortest path between two points along the surface of a sphere. Because Earth is roughly spherical, this is the natural way to measure how far apart two locations are. It is what airlines use when they quote flight distances, and what GPS units use when they tell you a destination is "240 km away as the crow flies".

The Haversine formula is the standard equation for great-circle distance. It is accurate to about 0.5% globally — more than enough for trip planning, real-estate searches, geofence design, and almost every consumer use case. Surveyors who need millimeter-level accuracy use the Vincenty formula instead, which accounts for Earth's slight equatorial bulge.

Great-circle distance is not the same as driving distance. Driving distance follows roads and is always longer; for an actual driving estimate, use a routing tool that knows about streets, one-ways, and tolls. Great-circle is also not the same as flight distance, which is great-circle plus winds and traffic-control re-routing.

A quick rule of thumb: 1° of latitude ≈ 111 km everywhere on Earth. 1° of longitude ≈ 111 km at the equator, shrinking to 0 at the poles. So two points with the same latitude differ in distance by roughly (longitude difference) × 111 × cos(latitude) km — a useful mental shortcut when you don't have a calculator.

Frequently asked questions

Why doesn't this match Google Maps' "distance" number?

Google Maps reports driving distance by default — the length of the route along roads. Our calculator reports the straight-line distance over the Earth's surface. For a Paris-to-London trip, driving is roughly 460 km, while great-circle is only about 344 km.

Can I enter DMS (degrees-minutes-seconds) here?

This tool takes decimal degrees only. Convert DMS to decimal degrees first using our coordinate converter, then paste the result here.

How accurate is the result?

The Haversine formula assumes a perfect sphere. Earth's true shape introduces about 0.3–0.5% error, so a 1000 km calculation might be off by 3–5 km. For sub-meter accuracy on long distances, use the Vincenty formula.

Does this work offline?

Once the page is loaded, the calculation itself runs entirely in your browser and works without a network. The map underneath needs to download tile images from OpenStreetMap, so it requires a connection to display.
